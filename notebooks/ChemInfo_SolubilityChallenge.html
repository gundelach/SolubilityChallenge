def akaike_inf_crit(k,y_test,y_pred):
    '''Calculates Akaikes information criterion given a number of params k, and a predicted and obsered data set'''
    residue = y_test - y_pred
    sse = (np.square(residue)).sum()
    return (2 * k + len(y_test) * math.log(sse))

def model_pred_eval(y_pred,y_test,name='Model1'):
    '''Calculates statistical measures of merit for predictions vs observations, returns Pandas DataFrame'''
    stats_dict={}
    error = abs(y_pred - y_test)
    stats_dict['mean_abs_error']=[np.mean(error)]
    stats_dict['median_abs_error']=[np.median(error)]
    stats_dict['median_accuracy']= [100 - np.median(100 * (error/y_test))]
    stats_dict['R2']=[r2_score(y_test,y_pred)]
    stats_dict['RMSE']=[math.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y_test,y_pred))]
    stats_dict['min_error']=[error.min()]
    stats_dict['max_error']=[error.max()]
    return pd.DataFrame.from_dict(stats_dict,orient='index',columns=[name])

Chemical Informatics: Assessed Work¶

Lennart Gundelach, June 2019, Oxford TMCS

Introduction¶

This assignment is about the 'Solubility Challenge', a challenge set by the cheminformatics community to predict the solubility of drug-like biomolecules based on a small, highly accurate data set of solubilities (Llina et. al.). Knowledge of the solubility of drug candidates is vital for effective drug development as insoluble drags are difficult to administer. Experimental determination is very challenging and hence there is a concerted effort do develop predictive models based for solubility based on structure information of biomolecules.

To build a qualitative structure property relationship model for solubility, descriptors must be defined and calculated for the biomolecules in the training and testing dataset. For this assessment a training and a test data set were provided. In addition to the solubilities, 10 molecular descriptors, calculated using Dragon software, were provided. The training set contains 85 molecules and the test set contains 28 molecules with solubility data. The solubilities are derived form experiment yet since no source was provided, the quality/reliability of the data is not known.

The training set is used to train statistical models. Their effectiveness is then tested on the test set. This ensures that the models are predictive, rather than simple reproducing the training set. None of the models in the training set may be in the test set. The training set is about $\frac{2}{3}$ of the data. This split allows a larger training set, at the expense of strength of validation the models via the test set. A small test set risks that 'non-conformist' molecules within may heavily skew the performance of predictive models. On the other hand, a small training set risks simplistic and non-representative models. A 50/50 split may provide a more balanced approach.

A method of validating the choice of split would be to re-split the data using the same ratio and re-testing the models developed below. Ideally, the performance of the models should be independent of the splitting of the data. If this is the case, the split is valid. If not, the data set must be expanded, or a more even split ratio employed. No information is given on how the split was implemented. The data split must be unbiased, and hence a random split via a specified ration is recommended. Advance knowledge of the test set may lead to a biased sampling of the training set via previous knowledge of the molecules and their properties. For example, known outliers may be purposefully ignored and well behaved molecules included.

The table below summarizes the 10 descriptors provided. ALOGP was used by Yalkowsky in early solubility models and will hence be an important descriptor. Yalkowsky also used melting point, which is strongly correlated to molecular weight for many molecules. Hence MW will also be an important factor (Ran et. al.). Sv and Si are also likely to be important as they are related to the inter-atomic forces the molecule would experience in solution. Intuitively the Hydrophilic factor should also be important, as it relates closely to the molecules interaction with water. A potential issue in the selection of descriptors is that they may be correlated. Especially MLOGP and ALOGP will be highly correlated and hence only one of the two measures should be employed. Alternatively methods like PCA and PLS can be used to form new derived descriptors which are orthogonal/independent.

Investigating linear correlation of each descriptor to $\log(S)$¶

We begin by analyzing to what extent each predictor is linearly correlated to the solubility. This will allow identification of potential candidate predictors for multiple linear regression. Below, the pair wise linear regressions are plotted in order to visually inspect the data. The logarithm of solubility, $\log(S)$, is used as the dependent variable as this produces significantly stronger correlation with the descriptors MW, Sv, Si, MLOGP and ALOGP. The R2 value for each linear regression is shown in the title of each plot. The R2 value is the coefficient of determination and describes the proportion of the variance in the dependent variable which is predictable from the independent variables. A value close to 1 indicates a strong fit of the linear regression model to the data set. In the plots below, none of the descriptors exhibit a strong linear correlation to solubility.

Principle component analysis on $\log(S)$¶

A major drawback of naive linear regression approaches as employed above is that the different descriptors may themselves be correlated. This can lead to poor performance of the linear regression, which assumes that all descriptors are independent. Principle component analysis (PCA) provides a means of negating this problem. PCA generates a set of new descriptors, called principle components, as linear combinations of the original descriptors, aiming to describe the maximum amount of variance in the data set with each principle component. The principle components are all orthogonal, i.e independent. Furthermore, only the leading principle components, describing most of the system variance, can be used, thus reducing the dimensionality of the problem. Below, a PCA analysis is set up and the first 8 principle components are used to build a principle component multiple linear least square regression model. Before constructing the PCs, the descriptors are scaled such that the mean of each descriptor is 0 and the standard deviation is 1. This allows a more balanced construction of the PCs than when the magnitudes of different descriptors vary greatly.

The PCR outperforms the simple multiple linear regresssion models both in predictive power and in ACI score. The R2 on the test set is 0.52 and almost equal to that on the training set. Furthermore, the RMSE is 2.17 log units, the best result so far. The PCR has only 8 parameters instead of the 10 of the over-parameterized multiple linear regression model, yet still outperforms it in every metric. This is also reflected by the lower ACI score. The scatter plot above of predicted verses actual solubilities in the test set shows a clear linear relationship.

However, there is an additional factor to consider. The choice of number of PCs is somewhat arbitrary and the use of different numbers of PCs results in a worse models as shown below. Using 10 principle components decreases the quality of the model, despite the inclusion of more principle components. This could indicate, that the PCR with 8 PCs performs better due to random effects, rather than a systematic improvement over the model with 10 PCs. Using either 8 or 10 PCs risks over-fitting the model however using less PCs lead to significantly worse models as not enough variance is covered.

This ties into the fact that we have access to the test set. This means that we can immediately check the validity of any model against the test set. Thus, the test set essentially becomes another training set because we use it to determine the best parameters (number of PCs) for the model. This would not be possible in a blind study in which the test set was not known in advance. As describe above, knowledge of the test sets allows fine tuning of the models that would otherwise not be possible and increase the risk of selecting a "lucky" model. On way to circumvent this problem would be to subdivide the training set into a smaller training and new test set. However, the training set provided here is much to small to employ this strategy. A build up or build down approach together with Akaike's information criterion would be a more systematic approach. ACI could also be applied on the training set in case the test set is not known.

Lastly, a major drawback of PCA is the loss of interpretability of the model. It is not straightforward to decompose the PCR into contributions from the individual original descriptors. Thus, chemical insights may be low in the use of PCA and related methods.

Partial least squares regression on $\log(S)$¶

Similar to PCA, the Partial least squares (PLS) method decomposes the descriptors into new descriptors (latent variables) by projecting the descriptors and solubility to a new space. Unlike PCA, PLS aims to capture the variance in both the dependent and independent variables via the latent variables. As for the PCR above, multiple linear regression using PLS performs inconsistently for different numbers of components/descriptors. The strongest predictive model is achieved at 7 components, but it is not obvious why. Again, this may be a result of random effects and by choosing this model, I may be falling victim to publication bias. The statistical measures are all very similar to those obatined by the PCR with 8 components. However the ACI score is noticeably better as one parameter less is used. The same considerations regarding the availability of the test set apply to PLS as they did to PCA.

Random Forest on $\log(S)$¶

So far, the PCR and PLS regression have produce the strongest models with comparable predictive power and ACI scores. In an attempt to improve upon these model, we try a completely different approach. Random Forest algorithms are a non-supervised ensemble learning method. During the training step, bootstrapping sampling techniques are used to generate "simulated ensembles" of observations from the training set. Each of these is used to train an individual decision tree. The predictive power of random forest method relies on averaging over the predictions of many decision trees. To avoid correlation between the individual decision trees, random subsets of descriptors are used in the individual decision trees. This method is popular as it essentially operates as a black box with few parameters and is noise resilient due to the averaging over many decision trees. The main drawback is the lack of interpetability of the resulting model. The individual trees make no meaningful predictions and it is difficult to extract physical meaning from the model.

Naive¶

First we implement a completely naive random forest regression on all unprocessed descriptors. The training set score is a measure of how well the algorithm has fit to the training data. A score close to 1 indicates a successful fitting. The RMSE value of the prediction of the naive random forest model is 2.80 which is worse than the PCR/PLS methods above as well as the simple multiple linear regression. The R2 score is notably low. This may be because the random forest method is not a linear model and thus the quality of its fit may not be best measured using a linear measure like R2. Furthermore, random forest method is non-parametric as it makes no assumptions about underlying probability distributions. This implies that non-parametric statistical tests would be required to properly probe the generated models. The RMSE is valid for non-linear models and will thus be used as the mean measure of quality for the random forest and neural net methods.

Neural Net with PCA¶

The last method attempted is the use of neural network based regression. This is a deep learning method which allows extremely complex, non-liner, functional forms to which to fit the data. The functional form is determined by the architecture of the neural net. The algorithm works by assigning many parameters to the complex functional form by minimizing against the training set. Due to the complexity of the minimization task, only local minima are found and hence the performance of the neural net will vary for each re-training. The number of parameters of the neural net must not exceed the number of observations, else the model will be over-fitted. The training set has 85 observations. Hence a neural net architecture with 8 PC inputs and three hidden layers of 4 hidden nodes each was chosen. The total number of parameters is $8\times 4 + 4 \times 4 + 4\times 4 + 4 \times 1 = 68$ parameters. The maximum iterations were set to 50000 to ensure convergence on the training set.

The table above summarizes the performance of the different models developed in the assignment. PLS with 7 descriptors produced the lowest RMSE on the test set and the highest R2 score. PLS7 also has the lowest ACI score due to it high quality fit and lower number of descriptors than PCA8. The simple multiple linear regression using only 4 descriptors performed quite well, and is the simplest and most easily interpretable model. Both PLS and PCA outperform it, however it is more difficult to assess which physical descriptors relate to solubility. The random forest method using PCA performed comparably to the 4 descriptor linear regression, however MLR4 is far more simple and reproducible. The neural network regression performed worst, and is also among the most difficult to interpret. The poor performance of the neural net is likely, in part, to my lack of experience working with neural nets. By optimizing the architecture of the set and employing bootstrap sampling, a better model could likely be build.

Using larger training and testing sets, other researchers have achieved models with RMSE of about 1 log unit and R2 of 0.7 (Boobier et al, 2017). In the solubility challenge itself, R2 scores of above 0.6 were achieved on of full test set (Hopfinger et. al.). Considering the small size of the training set provided and my limited experience with statistical modeling the RMSE of 2.0 and R2 of 0.58 achieved by the best model developed here is decent.
